7-Day Japan Travel Itinerary

Hakone Shrine・箱根神社

Start your first morning with a peaceful stroll through Hakone Shrine, nestled among towering cedar trees along the shores of Lake Ashi. The misty forest path leading up to the vibrant torii gate creates a serene, almost spiritual atmosphere — perfect for reflection and quiet photos. Don't miss the iconic red torii standing in the lake, offering a stunning view framed by water and mountains.

Torii of Peace・平和の鳥居

The Torii of Peace rises from the waters of Lake Ashi, symbolizing harmony between nature and spirit. This iconic red gate, standing serenely at the lake’s edge, offers a breathtaking photo spot with Mt. Fuji often visible in the distance on clear days.

The Ninety Steps・九十段

After admiring the serene view of the lake, climb the Ninety Steps, a moss-lined stone staircase that leads through a quiet cedar forest to Hakone Shrine’s inner grounds. Each step adds to the sense of anticipation, making the journey feel as sacred as the destination.

Worship Hall・拝殿

At the top of the Ninety Steps lies the Worship Hall, the spiritual heart of Hakone Shrine. Adorned with traditional architecture and rich in history, it’s a place where visitors offer prayers for safe travels, health, and good fortune. The quiet reverence in the air invites a moment of reflection.

Hakoneya・箱根屋

Just steps from Hakone Shrine, Hakoneya is a cozy local eatery perfect for a post-shrine meal. Known for its comforting, home-style Japanese dishes, this spot is a favorite among both locals and travelers. The katsudon—crispy pork cutlet simmered with egg over rice—is a must-try.

Lake Ashi Sightseeing Boat・芦ノ湖遊覧船

Set off from Moto-Hakone Port on the Lake Ashi Sightseeing Boat for a serene cruise across the lake. With comfortable seating and warm drinks in hand, enjoy views of forested hills, floating torii, and—on clear days—a stunning glimpse of Mt. Fuji. The round-trip ride offers a peaceful and scenic break in your day.

Enoshima・江ノ島

After your relaxing getaway in the mountains of Hakone, spend your late afternoon admiring the blue waters of Kamakura. Nearby, there is Enoshima, a charming island getaway just off the Shonan coast, connected to the mainland by a scenic bridge. Known for its coastal views, shrines, street food, and hidden caves, the island offers a perfect mix of nature, culture, and seaside fun. Climb to the top for panoramic ocean vistas—or explore the narrow alleyways lined with local shops and cafes.

Kamakura Kōkōmae Station・鎌倉高校前駅

Right before golden hour, take the local train to Kamakura Kokomae Station, a small seaside stop along the Enoden Line, famous for its picturesque ocean views and iconic railroad crossing. Beloved by anime fans and photographers alike, it's a peaceful spot where trains pass by with the sound of waves in the background—especially beautiful during sunset.

Ginza Kagari・銀座 篝 鎌倉店

Ginza Kagari’s Kamakura branch brings its renowned refined ramen to a relaxed, coastal setting. Known for its signature tori paitan (creamy chicken broth) ramen, the shop blends delicate flavors with beautifully presented bowls—perfect for a comforting yet elevated dinner after a long time of exploring. After you are done, take head to Kamakura Station and return to your hotel in Tokyo.

Minato Mirai・みなとみらい

Minato Mirai is Yokohama’s modern waterfront district, blending sleek skyscrapers with scenic bay views and laid-back attractions. Whether you're shopping at Queen's Square, strolling along the promenade, or riding the iconic Cosmo Clock Ferris wheel, the area offers something for everyone. With its mix of culture, entertainment, and seaside charm, Minato Mirai is the perfect place to spend a relaxed afternoon or romantic evening by the water.

Getting from Tokyo to Minato Mirai is quick and easy. Take the JR Tokaido Line or Yokosuka Line from Tokyo Station to Yokohama Station—a direct ride of about 25–30 minutes. From Yokohama, transfer to the Minatomirai Line and ride just two stops to Minatomirai Station, which drops you right into the heart of the waterfront district.

Shake Shack・シェイクシャック みなとみらい

Kick off your Yokohama adventure with a satisfying lunch at Shake Shack, located conveniently in the stylish Minato Mirai district. With floor to ceiling windows and views of the waterfront and skyline, it's the perfect casual spot to enjoy American-style burgers, crispy fries, and creamy milkshakes. The relaxed vibe and familiar comfort food offer a nice balance before jumping into the more adventurous parts of your day.

Yokohama Air Cabin・横浜エアカビン

After lunch, soar above the city on the Yokohama Air Cabin, a sleek urban ropeway connecting Sakuragicho Station to the waterfront promenade. As you glide across the skyline, you'll get a bird’s-eye view of the iconic Cosmo Clock Ferris wheel, the Red Brick Warehouse, and the shimmering bay. The ride only takes a few minutes, but the views—especially on a clear day—are a scenic highlight of Minato Mirai.

Cup Noodles Museum・カップヌードルミュージアム

Unleash your inner foodie and creative spirit at the Cup Noodles Museum, a fun and interactive space dedicated to one of Japan’s greatest inventions. Learn the story of Momofuku Ando and how instant ramen changed the world, explore quirky exhibits, and even design your own custom cup noodle to take home. It's educational, entertaining, and totally unique—perfect for travelers of any age.

Yokohama Cosmo World・よこはまコスモワールド

Wrap up the afternoon with a visit to Yokohama Cosmo World, a colorful amusement park along the bay. It’s home to the iconic Cosmo Clock 21 Ferris Wheel, which offers panoramic views of the city and ocean, especially magical at sunset. Try out the mini coasters or just enjoy the retro charm of the game arcades and carnival-style treats. No admission fee is required to enter—just pay per ride, making it a flexible and fun way to end your time in Minato Mirai.

Yokohama Chinatown・横浜中華街

Yokohama Chinatown the largest Chinatown in Japan and one of the most vibrant in the world, blending colorful architecture, rich history, and irresistible food into one dynamic neighborhood. Established in the mid-19th century as a hub for Chinese immigrants after Yokohama opened as an international port, it has since become a cultural crossroads where tradition and modern tourism meet. Beyond its famous street food—like xiaolongbao, sesame balls, and roasted duck—you’ll find ornate gates, Taoist temples like Kanteibyo, and bustling shops selling everything from herbal remedies to souvenirs. It’s not just a food destination, but a window into the deep-rooted Chinese influence on Yokohama’s cosmopolitan identity.

Yamashita Park・山下公園

Stretching along the Yokohama waterfront, Yamashita Park offers open lawns, flower beds, and sweeping views of the bay. Originally built from the rubble of the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake, the park is now a peaceful place to relax, watch ships go by, or stroll along the seaside promenade. With nearby attractions like the Hikawa Maru and the Red Brick Warehouse, it’s a lovely green space tucked between city life and the harbor.

Shichifuku・横浜中華街小籠包専門店 七福

Eat delicious xiaolongbao (pork soup dumplings) in Yokohama’s Chinatown, which has one of the richest Chinese food scenes in Japan. Shichifuku specializes in freshly steamed, juicy xiaolongbao with delicate wrappers and rich, savory broth. It's a cozy stop with fast service—perfect for grabbing a quick bite between exploring the vibrant streets and shops of Chinatown.

Fushimi Inari Taisha・伏見稲荷大社

The shrine sits at the base of a mountain, also named Inari, which is 233 meters (764 ft) above sea level, and includes trails up the mountain to many smaller shrines which span 4 kilometers (2.5 mi) and take approximately 2 hours to walk up all the way. This shrine is always crowded by tourists due to its major attraction: The Thousand Red Gates, so it is best to be there early (at 7 to 8 AM). There is no need to go to the end, but if you would like there should be a view of Kyoto on the peak.

Yasaka Pagoda・八坂の塔

The Yasaka Pagoda, also known as the Tower of Yasaka, is a Buddhist pagoda located in Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto, Japan. The 5-story tall pagoda is the last remaining structure of a 6th-century temple complex known as Hōkan Temple. The pagoda is now a tourist attraction.

This alleyway shown in the photo is also usually crowded by tourists, so if you want any clear photo opportunities it is best to go early. If clear photos aren’t that important, it may be more convenient to go at the same time we are planning to head to Kiyomizu temple, because they are essentially a few minutes away from one other.

Note that this is located in the Gion District, which the Kyoto Government has restricted access for tourists in certain alleyways to prevent geisha harassment.

Kiyomizu Temple・清水寺

Kiyomizu-dera ("Pure Water Monastery") is a Buddhist temple located in eastern Kyoto, Japan. The temple is part of the Historic Monuments of the Ancient Kyoto UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The popular expression "to jump off the stage at Kiyomizu" is the Japanese translation of the English expression "to take the plunge". This refers to an Edo-period tradition that held that if one were to survive a 13-meter (43-foot) jump from the stage, one's wish would be granted.

There is a good view of the city and visitors can take a sip of mountain spring water from Otowa Waterfall 音羽の滝 using the long scoops provided at the site. Hold out the long scoops, which has been pre-sterilized in UV blue light, to catch one of the 3 streams of spring water. Each stream is said to bestow longevity, success, and love. Go early, as the queues for Otowa Waterfall can get windingly long.

Heian Shrine・平安神宮

The Heian-jingu Shrine is a Shinto shrine located in Sakyō-ku, Kyoto, Japan. The Shrine is ranked as a Beppyō Jinja (別表神社), meaning it is one of the most important shrines in all of Japan.

The history is rooted in the fact that it was built to commemorate the 1100th anniversary of Heian-kyo (平安京, Japan’s Old Capital) and honor Emperor Tenmu, the emperor that ruled over Kyoto at that time.

The Japanese-style garden in the land of Heian Shrine takes up about half the land area (approximately 33060 meters-squared). It consists of beautiful sakura and maple trees, along with many ponds and flowers.

Nidec Kyoto Tower

ニデック京都タワー

Take a view on the top of Kyoto Tower around sunset of the city. Tickets are relatively cheap, only around $6.00 USD. There are many dinner options nearby before heading back to the hotel.

The train ride from Kyoto to Nara—specifically Kintetsu-Nara Station (近鉄奈良駅) takes around 45 minutes one way, and there is usually one train every 20 minutes. Assuming we arrive at the station at around 9 AM, we can safely get out of Nara Station at 10:30 AM.

Tonkatsu Ganko Nara・とんかつがんこ奈良店

This is a famous Tonkatsu restaurant that has a very high rating, opens at 11 AM on Mondays. This restaurant is very popular, so it is a good idea to eat it earlier (i.e. close to when we arrive at the station) before exploring the rest of Nara.

Nakatanidou Mochi Shop Show・中谷堂

This shop is famous for its theatrical and traditional way of making mochi and has went viral over its captivating process of rhythmic shouting and hammering mochi on a wooden mortar. The line for the mochi is usually quite long, but it moves quite fast. However, the view for the mochi making may be more crowded and hard to see during peak times.

Kofuku-ji・興福寺

Kofukuji used to be the family temple of the Fujiwara, the most powerful aristocratic clan during much of the Nara and Heian Periods. At 50 meters, the five-storied pagoda is Japan's second tallest wooden pagoda, just seven meters shorter than the five-storied pagoda at Kyoto's Toji Temple.

The complex is quite large, encompassing a park, many buildings, and the famous Sarusawa Pond. This area is part of a large green space that connects the to the Asajigahara Enchi and then Nara Park, so we can take our time walking through it and enjoying the scenery.

Kuki Cake・空気ケーキ

Nara Park 奈良公園

Landscaped, public park with ancient temples & the Nara National Museum of Art, plus tame deer. This is Nara Park proper, but essentially just another green space connected with the other places explored earlier in the day. You can feed the deer at Nara Park (along with Asajigahara Enchi) with special deer food that you can buy in the area. The deer are very polite and will bow for you to feed them, but be careful as sometimes they will get aggressive when you don’t give them what they want.

Asajigahara Enchi・浅茅ヶ原園地

Asajigahara-enchi Park, part of the extensive Nara Park, is known for its diverse landscape and cultural heritage. Located on the southern side of the park, near the first Torii gate of Kasuga Taisha Shrine, this hilly area offers stunning views, especially around the Sagiike Pond where the Ukimido Pavilion stands.

This is also quite close to Nara Park proper, so there are many deers in this area as well.

Kasuga Taisha・春日大社

Kasuga Taisha is one of the most sacred sites in all of Japan. it attracts both devotees and tourists seeking an iconic Shinto experience. It was built during 768 AD, and is beautifully lit up at night. There is no entrance fee but it is a short walk (or hike) from Nara Park and the rest of the previously visited green space.

Wagyu Suji Curry・和牛スジカレー

This restaurant specializes in Japanese curry and omurice, and it receives very high ratings from both Japanese and foreigners. Check out their menu here: https://steak-cielbleu.com/

Shibuya・渋谷

SHIBUYA SKY・渋谷スカイ

Shibuya Sky is a 229 meter (751 feet) tall observation deck located on the 47th floor (top floor) of Shibuya Scramble Square Tower. The open-air deck offers a 360-degree panoramic view of Tokyo.

It is a place to take the most stunning photos, so make sure to bring you camera and phone. Unfortunately, tickets during the evening are almost always taken even 4 weeks in advance, so plan accordingly or enjoy the view during the day.

Shibuya Scramble Crossing ・スクランブル交差点

Shibuya Crossing, is a popular pedestrian scramble crossing in Tokyo. It is located in front of the Shibuya Station Hachikō exit and stops vehicles in all directions to allow pedestrians to inundate the entire intersection. Note that there is also a famous dog statue on one of the corners of the intersection.

This is right next to Shibuya Sky, so there will be almost no commute time between the two of them. There is also small cafes (also Starbucks) nearby where you can watch the crossing in action and get something to drink.

MEGA Don Quijote・メガドンキホーテ

This is Don Quijote’s main store, and is a go-to shopping destination for international tourists in Tokyo, offering a vast range of affordable items, from daily essentials to souvenirs. It's one of Tokyo's largest stores and offers tax-free shopping for overseas visitors.

This expansive discount store offers an array of products, ranging from essential daily goods to groceries, home appliances, brand-name products, medicines, and souvenirs, all at incredibly reasonable prices!

Check out YouTube videos to see all the things to buy! A majority of the products are all very high quality! Note that if you want a tax refund, you are not allowed to open the packaging until you leave Japan.

CAFE: dotcom space Tokyo

This cafe is located near our next location, and is in one of the most popular subdistricts—Harajuku. It is a few steps away from Takeshita Street, a popular point in Harajuku that features a variety of fashion and wacky, youth-driven shops selling colorful and crazy clothes and accessories.

The cafe itself features very aesthetic and delicious foods, and is a good place to rest before continuing on for the rest of the night. Note that this cafe closes at 6:30 PM, in case of any change to the day itinerary.

Meiji Shrine 明治神宮

Meiji Shrine (Meiji Jingū) is a shrine dedicated to the deified spirits of Emperor Meiji and his consort, Empress Shoken. Located just beside the JR Yamanote Line's busy Harajuku Station, Meiji Shrine and the adjacent Yoyogi Park make up a large forested area within the densely built-up city and offer walking paths for a relaxing stroll away from the concrete.

Meiji Shrine closes at sunset, which is around 7:00 PM or later. It has no entrance fee so you are able to come and go as you wish. Due to its location next to the JR, it is very convenient to commute to Shinjuku, which is where the next set of locations are.

Shinjuku・新宿

Shinjuku is one of Tokyo’s busiest and most dynamic districts, known for its dazzling mix of skyscrapers, shopping, nightlife, and local flavor. From the neon-lit streets of Kabukicho to peaceful spots like Shinjuku Gyoen, it’s a place where ultra-modern Tokyo and traditional pockets coexist. Whether you're seeking ramen, bars, fashion, or just the energy of the city, Shinjuku is a must-see urban playground.

Cross Shinjuku Vision・東新宿ビル クロス

Right outside the East Exit of Shinjuku Station, Cross Shinjuku Vision is an electronic billboard famous for its 3D effect. The most famous appearance on the billboard is the cat, which is pretty much an icon of Tokyo itself.

There is also a famous Cat Cafe in the building (hence the giant cat), and if you want to check that out the hours are from 1 to 9 PM. The area is surrounded by many fashion shops, restaurants, and other things that are sure to fill your time in the evening. However, doing so will cut into the time for the next area, which is similar in that it is mostly shopping. Feel free to adjust the time in each area to your liking.

Central Road・セントラルロード

Central Road in Kabukicho is the brightest and most iconic area of Shinjuku, glowing with neon lights, massive billboards, and nonstop energy. Known for its nightlife, entertainment, and endless photo ops, it’s where you'll find everything from host clubs and arcades to cinemas and ramen joints. A stroll down this street captures the electric pulse of Tokyo after dark.

Tokyo Metropolitan Government Observation Decks

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building offers free observation decks with panoramic views of the Tokyo skyline. Located in West Shinjuku, the twin towers rise 243 meters high, giving you a stunning look at the cityscape—on clear days, even Mt. Fuji is visible. It’s a peaceful, budget-friendly spot to take in Tokyo from above, day or night.

Omoide Yokocho

Omoide Yokocho is a maze of narrow alleyways near the West Exit of Shinjuku Station, famous for its retro charm and smoky izakayas. Also known as Memory Lane, it offers a glimpse into postwar Tokyo, packed with tiny bars and eateries serving grilled skewers, sake, and comfort food. It’s the home of many long-running establishments where locals and tourists alike gather shoulder to shoulder for a taste of old Tokyo.

Minato Ward・港区

Tokyo Tower ・東京タワー

An icon of Tokyo’s skyline, Tokyo Tower stands 333 meters tall and glows bright red at night. Visit the observation decks for sweeping views of the city, and don’t miss the foot of the tower where you’ll find shops and quirky exhibits. Among the base of the tower, there is the esports center Tokyo Tower RED, which features many interactive games and physical activities.

TeamLab Borderless

Step into a world of digital wonder at teamLab Borderless, an immersive art museum where light, color, and motion blend seamlessly. Located in Azabudai Hills (formerly in Odaiba), it’s a surreal and unforgettable experience that’s perfect for photos and reflection.

Ginza & Chiyoda・銀座と千代田

Ginza’s 12-Story UNIQLO & Shopping District

Ginza is Tokyo’s luxury shopping hub, lined with flagship stores, upscale boutiques, and elegant cafes. Don’t miss the 12-floor UNIQLO megastore or the mesmerizing Art Aquarium Museum, where goldfish swim through artful, illuminated displays.

Imperial Palace・皇居

Home to Japan’s Emperor, the Imperial Palace is surrounded by peaceful moats, stone bridges, and gardens. While the inner grounds are rarely open, the East Gardens offer a beautiful and historic escape right in the heart of the city.

Taito & Sumida・台東と墨田

Tokyo Skytree・東京スカイツリー

Soaring 634 meters into the sky, Tokyo Skytree is the tallest structure in Japan and a striking symbol of modern Tokyo. Its sleek design blends cutting-edge engineering with traditional aesthetics, making it a must-see for architecture lovers and sightseers alike. Ride up to the two observation decks for breathtaking, 360-degree views that stretch as far as Mt. Fuji on a clear day. At the base, explore the bustling Solamachi complex, filled with shops, restaurants, and even an aquarium—perfect for spending a full afternoon above and below the clouds.

Asakusa Temple・浅草寺

Tokyo’s oldest and most famous temple, Sensoji in Asakusa is a must-visit for its giant red lantern, vibrant shopping street (Nakamise-dori), and spiritual atmosphere. It's a place where centuries of tradition meet lively modern tourism.

Akihabara・秋葉原

Known as Tokyo’s mecca for electronics, anime, and gaming, Akihabara buzzes with energy. Explore maid cafes, retro game shops, and multi-story arcades, or dive into the otaku culture that makes this district truly one of a kind.
